Traffic Fatality Analysis and Forecasting Using Statistical Modeling

Background

This project analyzed 25 years of traffic fatality data from 48 U.S. states (1980-2004) to examine the impact of various traffic laws, such as blood alcohol concentration (BAC) limits, seat belt regulations, and speed limits, on road safety. The dataset, sourced from national and state agencies, included detailed information on demographics, economic indicators, and policy changes. By leveraging this rich dataset, the project aimed to uncover the relationships between legislative interventions and traffic fatality rates, providing valuable insights for policymakers to enhance road safety.

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Used graphical (scatter plots, histograms, box plots) and tabular methods to uncover relationships between traffic fatality rates and explanatory variables (e.g., blood alcohol limits, seat belt laws). Identified patterns, outliers, and trends in the dataset spanning 25 years across 48 U.S. states.

Linear Regression Models and Fixed Effects Modeling

Built preliminary and expanded Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) models to assess the relationship between traffic fatalities and year-over-year changes, incorporating laws like blood alcohol concentration (BAC) limits and seat belt regulations. Applied transformations (log) to variables such as population and vehicle miles driven to handle skewness and improve model performance.

Estimated panel data models with state-level fixed effects to control for unobserved heterogeneity between states (e.g., geography, culture) that could affect traffic fatalities. This allowed for the isolation of the effects of changes in laws over time while accounting for state-specific factors.

Forecasting and Model Validation

Utilized regression models to forecast future traffic fatality rates, predicting trends based on changes in traffic laws, demographic shifts, and economic indicators, providing actionable insights for policymakers.
